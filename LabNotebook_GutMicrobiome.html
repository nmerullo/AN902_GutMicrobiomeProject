Noninvasive genetic sampling of endangered muriqui (Primates, Atelidae): efficiency of fecal DNA extraction.

Chaves, P. B., Paes, M. F., Mendes, S. L., Strier, K. B., Louro, I. D., & Fagundes, V. (2006). Noninvasive genetic sampling of endangered muriqui (Primates, Atelidae): efficiency of fecal DNA extraction. Genetics and Molecular Biology, 29(4), 750–754.

Background: The genus Brachyteles represents the largest neotropical nonhuman primate with two endemic species found in the Brazilian Atlantic Rainforest: B. hypoxanthus (northern muriqui) and B. arachnoides (southern muriqui). Due to habitat fragmentation and deforestation, muriquis have been classified as endangered since 1982 and critically endangered since 2000. Molecular genetic analyses have been limited by sample availability, but recent advancements allow for DNA extraction from fecal samples.

This study aimed to test the reliability of fecal DNA for PCR applications in muriquis. Fecal samples from 28 individually identified muriquis were collected and processed for DNA extraction. Various factors affecting PCR efficiency, such as DNA quality, quantity, and the influence of bovine serum albumin (BSA), were investigated. PCR amplification targeting a mitochondrial DNA segment was successful in most cases, indicating the potential of fecal DNA for genetic studies. Results showed that DNA quality and quantity significantly influenced PCR efficiency, with higher success rates observed in less degraded and higher quantity DNA samples. However, even degraded samples could yield positive results under optimal conditions. Interestingly, the addition of BSA did not significantly affect amplification efficiency in this study. This study also demonstrated the efficacy of using a cheaper DNA polymerase alternative and optimizing primer concentrations to minimize nonspecific PCR bands. The resulting PCR products were suitable for further analysis using Restriction Fragment Length Polymorphisms.

Conclusion: This study provides valuable insights into optimizing PCR reactions using fecal DNA from endangered muriquis, potentially reducing costs and time in genetic studies. The protocol developed may also apply to a wide range of primate and non-primate mammals in future genetic research projects.

Associations Between Nutrition, Gut Microbiome, and Health in A Novel Nonhuman Primate Model

Clayton, J.B., Al-Ghalith, G.A., Long, H.T., Tuan, B.V., Cabana, F., Huang, H., Vangay, P., Ward, T., Minh, V.V., Tam, N.A., Dat, N.T., Travis, D.A., Murtaugh, M.P., Covert, H., Glander, K.E., Nadler, T., Toddes, B., Sha, J.C.M., Singer, R., Knights, D., Johnson, T.J. (2018). Associations Between Nutrition, Gut Microbiome, and Health in A Novel Nonhuman Primate Model. Sci Rep, 8(1), 11159.

This study examines the fecal microbiomes of different populations of red-shanked doucs (Pygathrix nemaeus), a colobine primate species, living under various lifestyle conditions (wild, semi-wild, semi-captive, captive). This study aims to understand how lifestyle factors (diet and environment), influence the gut microbial communities of these primates, and how these microbial changes may relate to their health.

Background: The GI tract of primates, including humans, hosts trillions of bacteria that play crucial roles in various aspects of host health and behavior. While there has been considerable research on the human microbiome and microbiome studies in non-primate animals, less attention has been given to nonhuman primates, particularly concerning their lifestyle, diet, and gut microbiomes. The red-shanked douc is of particular interest due to its digestive characteristics, which combine foregut fermentation and hindgut digestion, resembling both humans and ruminant livestock. Additionally, doucs are endangered, and their failure to thrive in captivity, primarily due to severe gastrointestinal disease, presents a challenge.

Methods: Fecal samples were collected from doucs across all four populations. Samples were analyzed using DNA extraction, 16S rRNA PCR amplification, and Illumina MiSeq sequencing to characterize the fecal microbiomes.

Results:

·       Microbiome Diversity Decline: This study found a decline in fecal microbiome diversity from wild to captive environments, with the highest diversity observed in wild doucs and the lowest in captive doucs. Semi-wild and semi-captive doucs showed intermediate levels of diversity.

·       Alpha Diversity: Alpha diversity measures (e.g., Shannon diversity index), revealed that wild doucs had the most even microbiomes compared to semi-wild, semi-captive, and captive doucs.

·       Beta Diversity: Beta diversity analysis showed significant differences in gut microbiome composition between douc populations, indicating that each population had a unique microbiome influenced by their lifestyle.

·       Taxonomic Abundance Analysis: The fecal microbiomes of all douc populations were dominated by the phylum Firmicutes. However, there were differences in the abundance of certain taxa, with Bacteroidetes being more abundant in semi-captive and captive populations. Taxa such as Bacteroides and Prevotella showed a positive correlation with captivity level, while others like Adlercreutzia and Anaerostipes decreased with increasing captivity.

·       Firmicutes to Bacteroidetes Ratio: The ratio of Firmicutes to Bacteroidetes was highest in wild doucs and decreased with increasing captivity, suggesting a relationship between lifestyle and the microbial community’s energy harvest capacity.

·       Functional Analysis: Functional analysis using PICRUSt revealed shifts in microbial functions in captive doucs, with increased protein metabolism and decreased fatty acid metabolism compared to wild doucs. Certain pathways related to antibiotics and xenobiotic degradation were also associated with captivity.

Conclusion: This study provides valuable insights into the complex interplay between lifestyle, gut microbial composition, and health in doucs, with potential implications for understanding similar relationships in humans and other animal species.

The gut microbiome of nonhuman primates: Lessons in ecology and evolution

Clayton, J.B., Gomez, A., Amato, K., Knights, D., Travis, D.A., Blekhman, R., Knight, R., Leigh, S., Stumpf, R., Wolf, T., Glander, K.E., Cabana, F., Johnson, T.J. (2018). The gut microbiome of nonhuman primates: Lessons in ecology and evolution. Am J Primatol, 80(6).

Introduction to Host-Microbiome Relationship: The relationship between animals and their microbiomes is ancient, beginning when multicellular organisms evolved in a biosphere dominated by microbes. This relationship is particularly notable in the gastrointestinal tract, where microbial populations are abundant, diverse, and crucial for host health. The gut microbiome aids hosts in various functions, such as extracting energy from food and maintaining immune responses.

Advancements in Microbiome Research: Research on host-associated microbes traditionally relied on culture-dependent methods, which have limitations in identifying diverse microbial taxa. Culture-independent methods, such as DNA sequencing, have since revolutionized microbial ecology by providing a more comprehensive view of microbial communities. High-throughput sequencing platforms enable the analysis of large numbers of samples simultaneously, advancing our understanding of microbial diversity.

Challenges in Microbiome Research: Despite these advances, challenges persist in gut microbiome research, including biases introduced during sample collection, DNA extraction, and PCR amplification. Additionally, the choice of sequencing region and bioinformatics methods can influence results. Shotgun metagenomics offers an alternative approach that sequences all genetic material present in a sample, providing insights into the functional capabilities of gut microbial communities.

Regional Variations in Microbiota Composition: Feces are the most used material for studying the gut microbiome, but they may not fully represent microbial communities throughout the GI tract. Regional variations in microbiota composition have been observed, suggesting that fecal samples may not accurately reflect microbial populations in other gut regions.

Importance of Studying Primate Gut Microbiome: Understanding the primate gastrointestinal microbiome is essential for advancing knowledge of primate ecology, evolution, and health. Improved methodologies and interdisciplinary approaches will continue to enhance our understanding of host-microbiome interactions and their implications for animal biology and human health.

·       Evolutionary Significance: NHPs are crucial for understanding human evolution due to their genetic similarity to humans. The gut microbiota’s symbiotic relationship with the host likely played a significant role in dietary adaptations, influencing primate specialization and gut physiology.

·       Behavioral Implications: There is evidence suggesting a link between gut microbiota and nervous system function, with potential implications for stress-related behavior and psychiatric disorders. However, understanding gut-brain communication in NHPs remains limited.

·       Conservation Concerns: Human population growth and activities such as deforestation pose significant threats to NHP habitats and populations, affecting their ranging patterns and increasing the risk of disease transmission. Rescue centers aim to rehabilitate and release NHPs affected by habitat loss, but captive diets may negatively impact gastrointestinal microbiomes, affecting health and welfare.

Future Directions: Large-scale studies with both cross-sectional and longitudinal samples are needed to further advance our understanding of primate gut microbiomes. Functional assessments of microbial communities and collaboration among researchers are crucial for establishing standard procedures and sharing comparable datasets across multiple species and ecological niches.

Comparison of DNA extraction methods for 16S rRNA gene sequencing in the analysis of the human gut microbiome

Elie, C., Perret, M., Hage, H., Sentausa, E., Hesketh, A., Louis, K., Fritah-Lafont, A., Leissner, P., Vachon, C., Rostaing, H., Reynier, F., Gervasi, G., & Saliou, A. (2023). Comparison of DNA extraction methods for 16S rRNA gene sequencing in the analysis of the human gut microbiome. Scientific Reports, 13(1), 10279.

The reliability of metagenomics methods crucial for studying these microbial communities is often compromised by errors at various workflow stages, including sample collection, DNA extraction, library preparation, sequencing, and data analysis. To overcome these challenges and facilitate the integration of metagenomics methods into clinical practice, standardized procedures are urgently needed. Although some standardized protocols exist, the field of nucleic acid preparation is rapidly evolving, requiring regular protocol benchmarking. The choice of DNA extraction method significantly impacts bacterial community detection, making it a pivotal step in metagenomic studies.

This study evaluated four commercially available DNA extraction methods using 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing. These protocols underwent assessment based on both wet-lab and dry-lab criteria, including DNA yield, fragment size, purity, observed microbial diversity, accuracy in extracting Gram-positive bacteria, protocol repeatability, and overall performance. This study encompassed fecal samples from healthy volunteers, patients with Clostridium difficile infection (CDI), and a defined mixture of bacterial species (mock community) for accuracy assessment.

Results revealed significant variability in DNA extraction yield, fragment size, and purity among the tested protocols. Notably, the use of an upstream stool preprocessing device (SPD) improved DNA yield for some protocols and enhanced DNA purity for most. Additionally, combining SPD with DNA extraction protocols increased microbial diversity, particularly in recovering Gram-positive bacteria, as indicated by the Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio. The accuracy of extraction protocols was further validated using a mock community, with improved predictions observed when SPD was employed. Protocol repeatability was also enhanced with SPD integration.

Overall, this study identified the DNeasy PowerLyzer PowerSoil kit (S-DQ) as the top-performing protocol, demonstrating superior performance across multiple criteria. While other protocols showed similarly good results for specific criteria, S-DQ consistently stood out as the most effective. These findings underscore the critical role of protocol selection in metagenomic studies and highlight the potential benefits of incorporating standardized preprocessing methods, such as SPD, to enhance DNA extraction efficiency and microbial community analysis.

Improving the standards for gut microbiome analysis of fecal samples: insights from the field biology of Japanese macaques on Yakushima Island

Hayakawa, T., Sawada, A., Tanabe, A. S., Fukuda, S., Kishida, T., Kurihara, Y., Matsushima, K., Liu, J., Akomo-Okoue, E.-F., Gravena, W., Kashima, M., Suzuki, M., Kadowaki, K., Suzumura, T., Inoue, E., Sugiura, H., Hanya, G., & Agata, K. (2018). Improving the standards for gut microbiome analysis of fecal samples: insights from the field biology of Japanese macaques on Yakushima Island. Primates, 59(5), 423-436.

This study explores optimizing the analysis of gut microbiomes in wild Japanese macaques (Macaca fuscata) and captive ones, recognizing the significant impact of gut microbiome adaptation on various aspects of host biology. Employing next-generation sequencing based on the bacterial 16S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) gene, the research aimed to address the challenges posed by traditional fecal microbiome analysis techniques, which may not be directly applicable to wild nonhuman primates due to sample degradation, bacterial growth, and lack of laboratory facilities in remote habitats.

To tackle these challenges, this study explored various methods of sample storage, including freezing, stabilization solutions like RNAlater, and lysis buffer, to preserve fecal DNA effectively. By collecting fecal samples from both captive and wild Japanese macaques, this study compared different storage methods’ effectiveness in preserving fecal DNA. DNA extraction, purification, library preparation, and amplicon sequencing were conducted following established protocols, with operational taxonomic unit (OTU) picking and taxonomic identification characterizing the gut microbiome composition.

Results:

·       Experiment 1: Evaluated the effects of various sample storage and DNA extraction methods on captive Japanese macaque fecal samples, revealing some differences in OTU numbers and alpha diversity between storage methods but overall consistency in bacterial composition.

·       Experiment 2: Extended these findings to wild Japanese macaque fecal samples, observing slight variations across different storage methods but consistent microbiome profiles overall.

·       Experiment 3: Focused on comparing microbiome analysis results obtained from sequencing different regions of the 16S rRNA gene (V1-V2 vs. V3-V4), showing that the choice of sequencing region influenced the number of OTUs obtained. However, the Shannon index, a measure of alpha diversity, remained similar between the two regions, underscoring the importance of primer selection in microbiome studies.

This study provides valuable insights into methodological considerations for studying gut microbiomes in wild primates, emphasizing the need for optimizing sample processing methods and careful primer selection for accurate microbiome analysis. These findings contribute to our understanding of host-microbiome interactions in natural habitats and pave the way for future research to explore the effects of environmental factors on microbiome composition in wild primate populations.

Preservation Methods Differ in Fecal Microbiome Stability, Affecting Suitability for Field Studies

Song, S. J., Amir, A., Metcalf, J. L., Amato, K. R., Xu, Z. Z., Humphrey, G., & Knight, R. (2016). Preservation Methods Differ in Fecal Microbiome Stability, Affecting Suitability for Field Studies. mSystems, 1(3).

This study aimed to investigate the effects of different storage methods on fecal microbiota composition over 8 weeks using 10 human and 5 dog donors. Four common preservatives (ethanol at 70% and 95% concentrations, RNAlater, OMNIgene Gut, and FTA cards) were tested under various temperature conditions, and the microbial communities were analyzed using 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing.

Small Storage Effects: Despite variations introduced by storage methods, these effects were small compared to differences between species and individuals. The variation between species (human and dog) and among individuals explained a significant portion of the total variation in microbial communities.

Preservation Methods and Community Shifts: Certain preservation methods, such as freezing and the use of FTA cards, OMNIgene Gut, and 95% ethanol, resulted in minimal changes in microbial community composition and diversity, even after 8 weeks. Unstabilized samples and those preserved with 70% ethanol showed larger changes, especially under heat or freeze-thaw conditions.

Stability of Preservation Methods: FTA cards and OMNIgene Gut showed minimal changes in microbial community composition and diversity over time, similar to fresh samples. RNAlater and 95% ethanol also performed well, although RNAlater showed slightly more changes under non-freezing temperatures over longer periods.

Taxonomic Biases Introduced by Preservation: Different preservation methods introduced biases in specific taxa. For example, FTA cards resulted in increased diversity of Firmicutes taxa, while 70% ethanol led to increases in Streptococcus and Haemophilus taxa. OMNIgene Gut showed the smallest immediate changes in taxa abundance.

Recommendations: This study recommends using 95% ethanol, OMNIgene Gut, or FTA cards for long-term storage, cautioning against the use of 70% ethanol or unfixed samples for extended periods. The recommended methods showed minimal changes in microbial communities, comparable to technical replicates, making them suitable for microbiome analysis even over several weeks.

Overall, this study highlights the importance of selecting appropriate preservation methods for fecal samples in microbiome research, especially for long-term storage and field trials, to ensure minimal alterations in microbial community composition and accurate interpretation of results.

Stool sampling and DNA isolation kits affect DNA quality and bacterial composition following 16S rRNA gene sequencing using MiSeq Illumina platform

Videnska, P., Smerkova, K., Zwinsova, B., Popovici, V., Micenkova, L., Sedlar, K., & Budinska, E. (2019). Stool sampling and DNA isolation kits affect DNA quality and bacterial composition following 16S rRNA gene sequencing using MiSeq Illumina platform. Sci Rep, 9(1), 13837.

Large cohort studies are essential for comprehensively understanding the microbiome’s role, but they encounter obstacles related to sampling procedures and methodological variability. User-friendly sampling protocols are crucial for participant compliance, while methodological differences across studies can hinder cross-study comparisons.

To address these challenges, this study systematically assessed the effects of sampling and DNA isolation kits on bacterial DNA quality, diversity, and composition while considering user acceptance. Sixteen volunteers used three different sampling kits: stool container, flocked swabs, and cotton swabs. DNA was extracted using two isolation kits: PowerLyzer PowerSoil DNA Isolation Kit (PS) and QIAamp DNA Stool Mini Kit (QS).

The stool container emerged as the most acceptable sampling option, while the QS kit yielded significantly higher DNA yields compared to the PS kit. However, the PS kit produced DNA of better integrity. The addition of a bead-beating step to the QS protocol did not improve DNA integrity, suggesting other factors at play.

The presence of PCR inhibitors persisted in DNA samples after isolation with both kits, with stool container samples exhibiting lower proportions, indicating potential advantages for certain applications. Bacterial diversity analysis revealed that the PS kit yielded a higher number of observed OTUs, particularly when combined with the stool container sampling kit. The final bacterial composition was more influenced by the DNA isolation kit than the sampling kit, with the PS kit showing a preference for Gram-positive bacteria. This study underscores the importance of considering batch effects arising from the choice of isolation and sampling kits when comparing results between studies.

This study highlights the significant effects of DNA isolation and sampling kits on DNA quality and bacterial composition, emphasizing the need for standardized methodologies and careful consideration of batch effects in microbiome research.

Host genetics influence the gut microbiome.

Cortes-Ortiz & Amato. 2021. Host genetics influence the gut microbiome. Science 373(6551): 159-160.

[note: this publication is not the primary report of this study]

This study found some heritability of gut microbiome traits in yellow baboons (admixed with anubis baboons) living in Amboseli, Kenya. Such traits include the presence or relative abundance of certain taxa and the overall composition of the gut microbiome as measured from fecal samples. The researchers analyzed an unprecedented sample size of 858 individuals over 14 years, and they posited that previous studies have underestimated microbiome heritability due to small sample sizes. Nonetheless, more variation in gut microbiome traits was attributable to environmental factors. Heritability estimates also varied with environmental factors including season, diet, and host age.

Monitoring the variation in the gut microbiota of captive woolly monkeys related to changes in diet during a reintroduction process.

Quiroga-Gonzalez et al. 2021. Monitoring the variation in the gut microbiota of captive woolly monkeys related to changes in diet during a reintroduction process. Scientific Reports 11: 6522.

Major Takeaway: Site conditions affect intestinal microbiota, especially variation in the microbiome. This effect is largely via dietary changes.

Three captive wooly monkeys were reintroduced to a wild habitat. Fecal samples from before and after release were used to measure the intestinal microbiome. Diversity (Shannon Index) was higher following release and lower again following recapture. The monkeys were supplemented food after release, and the more wild-origin food they consumed, the more diverse their intestinal microbiome was. Individual microbiome differences existing before release were maintained after release such that individuals had unique microbiomes regardless of site condition.

  • When conducting this protocol, always wear a suitable lab coat, disposable acrylamide gloves and protective goggles/glasses. For more information, consult the appropriate safety data sheets (SDSs) available from the product supplier.
  • To ensure proper sample identification, label sides and tops of the tubes. Processes used in this protocol may rub the labels off of the tubes, so it’s important to duplicate!

  • If Solution C1 has precipitated, heat at 60°C until the precipitate dissolves. Shake to mix Solution C4 before use.
